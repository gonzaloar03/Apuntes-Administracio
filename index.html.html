A1 Block A · Social Economy

Why Social & Circular Economy?

The starting point of the whole subject: the traditional growth model is straining against social inequality and planetary limits, so new ways of organising the economy emerge.

The growth problem & the Doughnut 1.a

Why measuring success by GDP alone has become a problem.

Summary
  • The conventional model equates development with infinite GDP growth, ignoring social inequality and environmental limits.
  • Three structural challenges: GDP-dependence, social inequality, and environmental impact / decoupling from planetary boundaries.
  • Kate Raworth's Doughnut Economy reframes the goal: meet everyone's needs (social foundation) without overshooting the planet's ecological ceiling.
  • These "new trends" integrate social responsibility, equity and environmental sustainability to create collective value and common welfare.
Full detail

Challenges of traditional economic growth

  • Dependence on GDP — success is measured by growth alone. The core question: does development mean infinite growth or welfare?
  • Social inequality — growth that excludes vulnerable populations. Is welfare really "for everyone"?
  • Environmental impact — degradation and climate change.
  • Decoupling from planetary boundaries — excessive use of natural resources; the "anything goes" mindset is not sustainable.

The Doughnut Economy (Kate Raworth)

An alternative to the traditional economy that prioritises sustainable and fair development, aligned with the values of the Social & Solidarity Economy (social justice, equity, respect for the environment). “Between social and planetary boundaries lies an environmentally safe and socially just space in which humanity can thrive.”

How to rethink the economy:

  • Redistribute resources — equitable access to basic needs.
  • Respect planetary boundaries — cut emissions, protect biodiversity, avoid pollution.
  • Prioritise human well-being over economic growth.
  • Build regenerative and distributive economies — local economies that recycle resources and share benefits.

In companies: firms apply doughnut principles by aligning their core activity with meeting everyone's needs within planetary limits, and by transitioning to business models that distribute wealth and resources fairly. The framework scales from cities to businesses.

The challenge of inequality 1.c

Disparities in access to resources, opportunities and power — and why they matter for the economy.

Summary
  • Inequality = disparities in access to resources, opportunities and power, at two scales: global and within countries.
  • Classic lenses: Max Weber (social class by wealth, power & prestige → stratification) and Karl Marx (capitalists who own the means of production vs workers who sell their labour).
  • Five types: social, economic, educational, gender, legal.
  • Measured with the Gini coefficient, derived from the Lorenz curve.

Two scales

  • Inequality within countries — income & employment gaps, gender discrimination, ethnicity / social class, educational and health inequality. Consequences: persistent poverty, social exclusion, conflict, reduced cohesion.
  • Global inequality — disparity between countries/regions, rooted in colonialism & resource exploitation and unequal global structures. Current drivers: unequal trade & value chains, unequal access to technology, education and healthcare.

Measuring it: Gini & Lorenz

The most common measure is the Gini coefficient, based on the Lorenz curve — a cumulative curve comparing the actual distribution of a variable (e.g. income) against a perfectly equal distribution.

Types of inequality

  • Social — different treatment by social position, economic situation, religion, gender, culture or sexuality (extreme poverty, job insecurity, discrimination, fiscal injustice…).
  • Economic — distribution of wealth; income gap between richest and poorest limits access to goods and services.
  • Educational — uneven access to education (e.g. millions of refugee children out of school).
  • Gender — wage gap, part-time rates, access to management, treatment of women as carers, political participation.
  • Legal — laws or courts favouring some over others (e.g. nationals vs refugees).

Consequences

  • Economic: lower sustained growth, less innovation.
  • Social: polarisation and discontent.
  • Environmental: poorer populations suffer climate impacts more.
  • Political: less stability, more conflict.

What is the Social Economy? 1.d.1

Private, autonomous organisations that put people and social purpose before capital — "one person, one vote".

Summary
  • Formally organised private entities, with decision autonomy and free membership, that satisfy members' needs through the market and distribute surpluses by activity, not capital → "one person, one vote."
  • Two subsectors (UN SCN-93 / European SEC-95): a market subsector (cooperatives, mutuals, labour societies) and a non-market subsector (associations, foundations — "non-profit institutions serving households").
  • Core principles: primacy of person over capital, voluntary membership, democratic control, conjunction of member & general interest, solidarity, management independence, surpluses for social purpose.
Full detail

The Social Economy is a set of formally organised private companies, with autonomy of decision and freedom of adhesion, created to satisfy the needs of their members through the market — producing goods and services and distributing benefits/surpluses (and decision-making) not according to capital, but to each member's activity. It also groups private entities producing non-market services for households whose surpluses cannot be appropriated by those who control or finance them.

General principles of social economy companies

  • Primacy of the person and the social purpose over capital.
  • Voluntary membership.
  • Democratic control.
  • Conjunction of members' interests and the general interest.
  • Defence of solidarity and responsibility.
  • Independence of management.
  • Most surpluses go to sustainable development, members' and the general interest.

The two subsectors

  • Market subsector — entities created to satisfy members' needs (members are usually also users), that operate as market producers selling at prices that cover most of their resources, and distribute surpluses by activity (not capital). Hard core: cooperatives, mutual societies, plus social-economy business groups, labour societies and many labour-insertion firms. Principle: "one person, one vote."
  • Non-market subsector — mainly associations and foundations serving third parties (families/households) at economically non-significant prices; resources from donations, fees and subsidies; surpluses cannot be appropriated by members. SEC-1995 calls them non-profit institutions serving households (NPISH). CIRIEC also admits, as an exception, the Third Sector of social action (non-democratic but providing preferential goods).

Theoretical roots 1.d.2

The intellectual currents behind the social and solidarity economy.

Summary
  • Karl Marx — critique of capitalism: alienating & insecure work, the theory of surplus value (profit = exploitation), endemic crises of abundance, commodity fetishism; wanted a classless society and redistribution.
  • Solidarity Economy — read three ways: a political alternative to capitalism, a plural/labour economy, or a specific type of firm based on democracy, self-management and collective entrepreneurship.
  • Social Market Economy (Müller-Armack, 1946; Ordoliberalism) — combines market freedom with social equity; markets allocate, institutions correct excesses.
  • Labour-movement influences: Anarchism (Bakunin), Communism (Engels, Lenin) — cooperatives as emancipatory tools.
Full detail

Karl Marx (1818–1883)

A fierce critic of capitalism. Key problems he identified:

  • Alienating work — in a specialised system, workers don't feel they contribute to society (a disconnect between thought and action).
  • Insecure work.
  • Theory of surplus value — workers earn less while capitalists get richer through "primitive accumulation"; profit is a euphemism for exploitation.
  • Endemic instability — recurring crises of abundance: we produce and consume more than we need.
  • Commodity fetishism — capitalism forces economic interest before all else and shapes ideology (anxious, competitive, conformist society).

Marx wanted a world without social classes, private property or inherited wealth — with progressive taxation, centralised banking, public education and healthcare, achieved by the working class taking power through revolution. His ideas shaped 20th-century movements (Russia, China, Cuba).

Solidarity Economy — three interpretations

  1. A political proposal of social transformation towards an alternative model to capitalism (open "transits", not closed models).
  2. A plural / labour / "matristic" economy where economic processes serve the reproduction of life under participatory, democratic processes (Laville, Coraggio, Arruda).
  3. A specific type of company based on democracy, self-management and collective entrepreneurship — often adopting classic Social Economy forms (cooperatives, mutuals, associations) but not exclusively.

Social Market Economy (Alfred Müller-Armack, 1946)

Markets are the best resource-allocation system, but institutions provide the ethical and social conditions for efficient and equitable operation, correcting excesses (it rejects the centrally planned economy). Its core is the "combination of market freedom with social equity": individual rights, republicanism and the market synthesised with social justice and solidarity. It is an open idea, not a closed theory (Ordoliberalism).

Labour-movement influences

  • Anarchism — Bakunin (1814–1876): cooperatives as emancipatory; equality, freedom, solidarity, bottom-up organisation (though he still saw revolution as inevitable).
  • Communism — Engels (1820–1895): agricultural cooperatives, especially for small owners. Lenin (1870–1924): ambivalent on cooperativism.
A2 Block A · Social Economy

Alternative Trends of Socio-Economic Development

Five responses to the limits of conventional capitalism — arranged from "reform from within" to "a different organising principle entirely".

How to read these five trends
  • CSR reforms the behaviour of conventional firms.
  • Natural Capitalism redesigns production around resource efficiency & ecosystem value.
  • Degrowth questions the idea that more output always means more welfare.
  • B-Corp is a certification framework combining profit with positive impact.
  • Social Economy organisations go furthest: purpose, participation and the primacy of people are built into their governance structure.

Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR) 2.a

Managing the impacts a company's activity has on all its stakeholders — as strategy, not charity.

Summary
  • CSR = directing a company by managing its impacts on clients, employees, shareholders, communities, the environment and society — a strategic asset, not just donations or image.
  • It evolved from philanthropy → labour/environmental concern → today's responsible business conduct (supply chains, human rights, due diligence).
  • Six dimensions: governance, workers, consumers, environment, community, value chain.
  • The key test: does CSR change core strategy, or only reputation? It now connects to double materiality, regulation and risk.
Full detail

CSR conceives the company as an institution responsible not only to shareholders but for the social and environmental impacts of its activity. The key question shifts from "How profitable is the company?" to "How does it create value, and at what social and environmental cost?"

Conceptual evolution

  • Roots in US Socially Responsible Investment (SRI) / ethical funds — originally religious groups avoiding "sin values" (alcohol, tobacco, gambling).
  • 1970s: anti-Vietnam-War groups avoided investing in arms-linked firms.
  • 1980s: social/environmental vision after disasters like Bhopal (1984) and Chernobyl (1986) highlighted negative externalities.
  • 1990s–2000s: labour exploitation cases (Nike, Zara in Asia), accounting fraud (Enron 2001) and bad finance (Lehman Brothers 2008) raised demand for ethical conduct.

CSR has moved from voluntary goodwill to more structured accountability.

Main dimensions

  • Governance — ethics, transparency, accountability, anti-corruption.
  • Workers — decent work, safety, equal opportunity, training, participation.
  • Consumers — quality, safety, truthful information, fair treatment.
  • Environment — emissions, waste, resources, life-cycle impacts.
  • Community — local development, social inclusion, territorial responsibility.
  • Value chain — responsible sourcing and supplier monitoring.

CSR creates trade-offs rather than easy moral choices (lower costs vs labour standards; speed vs sustainability; profit vs transparency). Today firms are expected to disclose how sustainability affects them and how they affect people and the planet — the idea of double materiality.

Natural Capitalism 2.b

Recognising nature as the capital that makes the economy possible.

Summary
  • Natural capital = natural resources + ecosystem services that make economic life possible; nature must not be treated as free and unlimited (Hawken et al., 1999).
  • Long-term prosperity depends on preserving the ecological systems that sustain production — so degradation is an economic, not only moral, problem.
  • Four shifts: (1) resource productivity & energy efficiency, (2) biologically-inspired / circular production, (3) products → solutions & services, (4) reinvest in natural capital (restore & regenerate).
Full detail

"Economic systems function within the biophysical limits of the planet, but act as if they do not exist" (Hawken et al., 1999). Natural capital — the stock of natural resources plus service flows — is rarely internalised in market prices. Natural Capitalism recognises the economy's dependence on natural resources and ecosystem services and proposes a transition to a model that values natural capital, human capital and ecosystem services rather than only money and goods. It is inspired by industrial ecology and systemic efficiency, and criticises traditional capitalism for ignoring environmental costs in its accounting.

The four shifts

  1. Increasing resource productivity / energy efficiency — radical efficiency (up to ~10×), e.g. building design cutting energy demand >80%.
  2. Biologically-inspired, circular production — closed-loop / cradle-to-cradle; reuse of materials; products designed to be 100% recycled or compostable (biomimetics).
  3. From products to solutions/services (servitisation) — e.g. leasing the service of light rather than selling bulbs → incentivises efficiency & durability; renting.
  4. Reinvest in natural capital — restoration and regeneration; investment in ecosystem health and community well-being; intergenerational equity.

In management practice

Firms reduce waste, energy and material intensity; redesign products to be durable, repairable and recyclable; adopt service-based models; and support ecosystem restoration tied to their long-term viability. Sustainability becomes part of innovation and competitive strategy.

Degrowth 2.c

"Living better with less" — questioning growth itself as the goal of affluent societies.

Summary
  • A critical perspective arguing unlimited GDP growth can't be the main goal on a finite planet; well-being doesn't depend on ever-more production and consumption.
  • It is NOT "negative growth" or a recession — it's a planned, equitable reduction of energy & material use in high-income (Global North) economies.
  • Origin: ecological economists/philosophers — Serge Latouche, building on Nicholas Georgescu-Roegen.
  • Key shift: not just how much we produce, but what, for whom and at what ecological cost; rejects planned obsolescence.
Full detail

As we saw with the Doughnut, capitalism equates development with continuous GDP growth. Degrowth critiques this. Its main aim is to address social and especially environmental problems by breaking the paradigm that welfare depends on growth, proposing to "live better with less" — a simpler lifestyle. "Degrowth is not a recipe for poverty, but an invitation to rethink our priorities and build fairer and more sustainable societies" — Serge Latouche.

What degrowth is — and isn't

  • It is not negative growth or recession.
  • It claims unlimited GDP growth cannot be the main goal of affluent societies on a finite planet.
  • It proposes a planned reduction in energy and material use, especially in high-income economies, to cut ecological damage and inequality.
  • It questions that "more output" always means "more progress": some production is socially necessary, other production mainly fuels consumerism and waste.

Practical examples

  • Planned obsolescence — fits growth-at-all-costs (constant demand for new goods); degrowth defends recycling, repair and reuse.
  • Food prices — most food is produced with negative net energy due to fossil-fuel dependence (fertilisers, pesticides, tractors), so prices trend up with fuel prices.
  • Critical minerals — extraction/processing is concentrated in China, which helps explain US–China trade and hegemony tensions.

Debates & criticism

Critics warn of tensions with employment, investment and fiscal policy. A central debate: is "green growth" enough, or must affluent economies also reduce total material and energy throughput? Degrowth opens a broader discussion on redesigning institutions and firms for a low-impact economy.

Don't confuseDegrowth (planned reduction, critical of green-growth) ≠ Doughnut/wellbeing economics (agnostic about GDP, works within capitalism) ≠ recession (unplanned contraction). See B1/B2 on post-growth.

B-Corp 2.d / 2.e

A certification that reforms capitalism from within — profit as a means to positive impact.

Summary
  • B-Corps (Benefit Corporations) meet high standards of social, environmental, transparency & accountability performance, balancing profit with positive impact.
  • Certified by B Lab (non-profit, founded 2006, USA) via the B Impact Assessment across five areas: Governance, Workers, Community, Environment, Clients.
  • Threshold: ≥ 80 / 200 points; recertify every 3 years.
  • It reforms capitalism from within rather than replacing it — the open question is whether it transforms the business model or mainly signals legitimacy.
Full detail

B Lab is the non-profit behind the B-Corp certification and the "B movement" (founded 2006, USA). It manages certification, develops tools and promotes legal changes; it began as a movement (much like the Doughnut today). A B-Corp is a company certified by B Lab for meeting verified standards of social, environmental and governance performance, designed to show it creates value not only for shareholders but for workers, communities and the planet — "business as a force for good".

Certification process (B Impact Assessment)

  1. B Impact Assessment — a questionnaire measuring impact in five areas: Governance, Workers, Community, Environment, Clients.
  2. Minimum score: at least 80 out of 200.
  3. Review & verification by B Lab.
  4. Ongoing commitment: recertification every 3 years to ensure continuous improvement.

Benefits

  • Positive reputation with ethical consumers.
  • Access to new markets & partners who value sustainability.
  • Employee engagement — motivation & retention of purpose-driven talent.
  • Competitive advantage / differentiation in saturated sectors.
  • Business resilience — readiness for stricter regulation (e.g. European Green Deal).

Critical question: does certification transform the core business model, or mainly improve legitimacy and signalling? B Lab's newer standards push for concrete, verifiable minimum requirements across ESG areas, making it an evolving accountability system rather than a mere label.

A3 Block A · Social Economy

The Economy for the Common Good

Christian Felber's proposal (ECG): re-orient the aim of the economy from financial profit to the common good — as a model, an institutional reform, and a social movement.

What is the Economy for the Common Good? 2.a / 2.b

Summary
  • Born from a systemic crisis in which the system often rewards negative outcomes — so marginal corrections aren't enough. Motto: TAPAS ("There Are Plenty of AlternativeS") instead of TINA ("There Is No Alternative").
  • ECG has a triple nature: a normative model, a process of institutional change, and a social movement.
  • It distinguishes oikonomia (money as a means → natural) from chrematistike (money as the aim → unnatural), and sees the economy as embedded in society, which is embedded in the ecosphere.
  • It replaces financial metrics with common-good ones and proposes the Common Good Balance Sheet.
Full detail

The starting point — a crisis landscape

Rising inequality, ecological stress and democratic disaffection have weakened trust in the current model. People feel a gap between economic performance and social well-being. The problem is not only that the system produces negative outcomes, but that it often rewards them — hence the argument that marginal corrections are insufficient. TAPAS instead of TINA: there is not just one possible economic order (social & solidarity economy, fair trade, ethical banking, degrowth, circular economy…). ECG belongs to this broader landscape and presents itself as a normative model + institutional change + social movement.

The core idea

  • Oikonomia — money as a way → "healthy, natural". Chrematistike — money as the aim → "unnatural".
  • "The property obliges. Its use must serve at the same time the common good" (Art. 14, German Constitution); "All the wealth of the country… is subordinated to the general interest" (Art. 128, Spanish Constitution).
  • The economy is embedded in human society, which is embedded in the ecosphere; economic activity can't be judged apart from social and ecological conditions.

Replacing the metrics

The Common Good Balance Sheet

An instrument to assess the ethical and social performance of organisations — how a firm affects workers, suppliers, customers, communities and the environment — making ethical claims measurable and comparable (the ECG Label). It is also an organisational-development tool: clarifying mission, improving internal practices, communicating values.

Benefits / incentives for firms with strong common-good performance: tax benefits, preferential public procurement, better credit conditions, closer links to public research support — aligning market incentives with social value.

Profit & money in the ECG 2.c / 2.d

Summary
  • Profit is a means, not the aim (Profit = way ≠ aim). It stays necessary for viability but should sustain productive activity, not be the ultimate goal.
  • Acceptable uses of surplus: socially/ecologically beneficial investment, repaying credit, reserves, distribution to workers, interest-free loans to co-companies.
  • Money is a public good: finance should serve the real economy — redirected to socially useful, ethically defensible investment.
Full detail

The notion of profit

Profit remains necessary for viability, but in Felber's view it should be treated as a means for sustaining productive activity, not the ultimate goal — which changes the acceptable uses of surplus:

  • Investments with social and ecological gain.
  • Repayment of credits.
  • Withholding of reserves.
  • Distribution to the people who work in the company.
  • Interest-free loans to co-companies.

Money as a public good

Money should be treated as a public good: finance must serve the real economy, redirected toward real, socially useful and ethically defensible investment (with a stronger public role for central banks).

A4 Block A · Social Economy

Types of Organisations

Comparing two ways of organising economic activity — and mapping the full family of Social Economy organisations.

Capitalist firms vs Social Economy 3.1

The question isn't only "what legal form?" but who owns, who decides, what's the purpose, how surpluses are shared, and who benefits.

Summary
  • In a capitalist firm the organising principle is capital ownership: ownership, voting and profit follow shares; workers participate via wages.
  • In the Social Economy the priority is people, collective needs and social purpose, not capital remuneration; governance is democratic and surpluses follow activity/work.
  • In Spain, Law 5/2011 sets the principles; the field splits (CIRIEC) into a market and a non-market subsector.

Spain — Law 5/2011 principles

  • Primacy of people and social purpose over capital.
  • Democratic, transparent and participatory management.
  • Allocation of results mainly by work, activity or social purpose.
  • Solidarity, local development, equality, quality employment, sustainability.
  • Independence from public authorities.

The 7 cooperative principles (ICA) — the basis of these organisations

  1. Voluntary and open membership.
  2. Democratic member control.
  3. Member economic participation.
  4. Autonomy and independence.
  5. Education, training and information.
  6. Cooperation among cooperatives.
  7. Concern for community.

Market Social Economy CIRIEC

Organisations that sell goods/services in the market but follow Social Economy principles.

Summary
  • Income comes mostly from sales/fees; they compete with conventional firms but ownership is collective/mutual/worker-based and surpluses follow activity, not capital.
  • The hard core: cooperatives (worker, credit/SACCO, consumer, agricultural) and mutual societies.
  • Also: Labour companies (S.A.L.), insertion companies and Special Employment Centres — market activity subordinated to a social-inclusion mission.
Full detail

Market Social Economy organisations differ from conventional firms because capital is not the central organising principle, ownership is collective/mutual/worker-based, governance is democratic, and surpluses are limited, reinvested or distributed by activity/work/member participation.

Cooperatives

  • Worker cooperatives — owned & democratically controlled by the people who work in them; main purpose is to create/maintain employment; workers are co-owners, so the capital–labour opposition is partly transformed.
  • Savings & credit cooperatives (SACCO / credit unions) — serve members' financial needs; usually local, suited to rural areas; non-profit legal entities (World Council of Credit Unions, WOCCU).
  • Consumer cooperatives — buy products/services to satisfy members' needs with the best quality and price.
  • Agricultural cooperatives — farmers share resources. Services cooperatives (most common) split into supply (inputs: seeds, fertiliser, fuel, machinery) and marketing (transform, package, distribute, sell produce). A separate type is community land exploitation (land & machinery pooled, members work jointly).

Labour company — S.A.L. (Sociedad Anónima Laboral)

Capital is divided into shares (like a corporation), but the majority of shares must belong to the working partners (with indefinite contracts) — closer to a cooperative than a capitalist firm. It can have non-working capitalist partners, working partners and non-member workers; hours of non-member workers are capped at 15% (25% in firms with <25 employees) of partners' hours, ensuring most work is done by partners.

Other market SE

  • Mutual societies — people-based organisations providing insurance, welfare or risk-protection to members, based on mutual aid and risk-sharing (not to remunerate external capital).
  • Insertion companies — support people at risk of social exclusion through employment, training and transition into the ordinary labour market.
  • Special Employment Centres — provide paid employment for people with disabilities and support their social and labour inclusion.

Non-Market (Solidarity) Social Economy CIRIEC

Non-profits providing services free or at non-significant prices, financed by funding, donations and fees.

Summary
  • Output is not governed by market exchange; income from public funding, donations, fees, grants, volunteers; surpluses can't be appropriated by members.
  • Main forms: associations (democratic, non-profit, independent) and foundations (an asset dedicated to a general-interest purpose, Law 50/2002).
  • Includes the third sector & singular entities — Cruz Roja, Cáritas, Grupo Social ONCE.
Full detail

Associations

  • Democratic — decisions made by the General Assembly of members (sovereign body); the Board of Directors / governing body represents and administers it.
  • Non-profit — profits can't be distributed to members; they must be reinvested in the association's objectives (it can carry out economic activity and hire people).
  • Independent — decisions depend solely on the members.

Foundations

The allocation of an asset to achieve a purpose of general interest (never particular) — the defining feature is the patrimony/heritage. Under Law 50/2002, purposes include human rights, social assistance & inclusion, education, culture, science, sport, health, development cooperation, environmental defence, promotion of the social economy, etc. Assets and surpluses must serve the general-interest purpose; founders can be natural or legal persons; the endowment must be adequate and sufficient.

Third sector & singular entities

Large social organisations serving households/vulnerable groups: social-action associations and foundations, Cruz Roja, Cáritas, Grupo Social ONCE and other non-profits providing social services, inclusion, humanitarian support, care, education, health and community development.

The five key questionsFor any organisation, ask: What is its purpose? Who controls it? How is it financed? How are surpluses used? Whose needs does it serve? Social Economy differs from capitalist firms (capital isn't dominant) and from the public sector (it's private & autonomous).
A5 Block A · Social Economy

Governance & Democracy

Governance is about power: who owns the organisation, who decides, in whose interest, and who is accountable. This is where the Social Economy differs most sharply from the capitalist firm.

Who holds power — and how decisions are made 3.2

Summary
  • Four governance questions: Who owns? Who decides? In whose interest? Who is accountable?
  • Capitalist firm: power follows capital — the General Meeting of Shareholders (votes ∝ shares) appoints a Board of Directors; the interest served is the shareholders'.
  • Social Economy: power follows membership — the General Assembly (one member, one vote) is sovereign and elects a Governing Council ("Consejo Rector"), with an oversight/intervention body controlling accounts; the interest served is members' + the general interest.
Full detail

  2. Who decides (and how is voting power allocated)?
  3. In whose interest is the activity run?
  4. Who is accountable, and to whom?

Circular Economy — Context

Why we need a circular economy: the intellectual roots, the global pressures, the physical limits of the planet, and the safe-and-just space of the Doughnut.

Economic thinking: Industrial Ecology & Biomimicry 1

Summary
  • Industrial Ecology (1989) studies material & energy flows through industrial systems to build closed loops where one process's waste is another's input — eliminating the idea of unusable by-products.
  • Biomimicry uses Nature as model, measure and mentor — designing as nature does, where "waste = food".
Full detail
  • Industrial Ecology (1989) — the study of material and energy flows through industrial systems, aiming to create closed-loop processes in which waste serves as input for another process, eliminating the notion of an unusable by-product.
  • Biomimicry — uses Nature for three fundamental principles: Nature as a model (imitate its forms/processes), Nature as a measure (use ecological standards to judge our designs) and Nature as a mentor (learn from, not just extract from, nature).

Environmental context: trends & risks 2

Summary
  • A trend is a general direction of gradual change; key global trends — resource scarcity, climate crisis, population growth — are drivers of change for business.
  • A global risk is the possibility of something bad affecting the whole planet; risks & trends interrelate across five domains: geopolitical, economic, environmental, social, technological.
Full detail

A trend is a pattern of gradual change resulting from shifts in attitudes, values, technologies and the economic landscape; business trends drive strategic decisions. Major global trends: resource scarcity, climate crisis, population growth.

The five risk domains

  • Geopolitical — opportunities & tensions between states.
  • Economic — financial/monetary systems affecting trade, manufacturing, supply chains.
  • Environmental — hazards to human health, plant & animal life, natural resources.
  • Social — long-range effects of catastrophes (infrastructure collapse, disasters, industrial accidents).
  • Technological — technical failures, compromised data, poor resource management, cyberattacks.

Limits to growth & planetary boundaries 3

Summary
  • Our culture equates consumerism with progress, measured by GDP — but high consumption doesn't significantly raise life satisfaction (Paul Ekins).
  • The linear economy = TAKE → MAKE → USE → DISPOSE, generating mounting social & environmental externalities on a finite planet.
  • Limits to Growth (Club of Rome / MIT, 1972) first questioned indefinite growth.
  • Planetary boundaries (Rockström et al., 2009): nine "safe operating space" limits; crossing a threshold (tipping point) risks abrupt, possibly catastrophic change.
Full detail

Consumerism & the meaning of "progress"

Contemporary culture is built on consumption; consumerism is treated as a symbol of success. Paul Ekins described it as "the possession and use of an increasing number and variety of goods and services" as the surest perceived route to happiness, status and national success — yet high consumption does not significantly increase life satisfaction. Progress is measured by GDP, and the objective becomes sustained GDP increase, driving competition for resources and rising environmental, economic and social impacts.

The linear economy

A socio-economic system based on producing goods from extracted raw materials and subsequent consumption: TAKE (extract & refine resources) → MAKE (fabricate products) → USE (consumption) → DISPOSE (waste). Over time this creates growing social & environmental externalities and dependencies/risks.

Limits to Growth (1972)

A report commissioned from MIT by the Club of Rome, published in 1972 just before the first oil crisis. It first questioned indefinite economic growth, concluding that if population, industrialisation, pollution, food production and resource exploitation keep growing unchanged, the planet will reach the absolute limits of growth within ~100 years, regardless of technology.

Planetary boundaries (Rockström & Steffen, 2009)

A "safe operating space" framework from the Stockholm Resilience Centre. A boundary marks the safe zone; a threshold / tipping point is the value at which a small change triggers large, possibly catastrophic, non-linear change. The nine boundaries:

  1. Climate change
  2. Biosphere integrity (biodiversity loss)
  3. Novel entities (e.g. synthetic chemicals, plastics, GMOs, nuclear waste)
  4. Stratospheric ozone depletion
  5. Atmospheric aerosol loading
  6. Ocean acidification
  7. Biogeochemical flows (nitrogen & phosphorus)
  8. Freshwater change
  9. Land-system change

Doughnut economics 4

Summary
  • Kate Raworth: between a social foundation (everyone's basic needs met) and an ecological ceiling (planetary boundaries) lies a safe and just space for humanity to thrive.
  • The goal of a healthy economy is to thrive, not (necessarily) grow.
Full detail

"Between social and planetary boundaries lies an environmentally safe and socially just space in which humanity can thrive." The inner ring is the social foundation (the minimum everyone needs — food, water, health, education, etc.); the outer ring is the ecological ceiling (the planetary boundaries). The aim is to keep humanity in the doughnut: meeting everyone's needs without overshooting the planet — designing the economy "to thrive, not to grow". (Cross-reference: A1 covers the Doughnut from the Social-Economy side.)

Applying the Circular Economy — Models & Strategies

From the linear "take-make-dispose" logic to circular principles, the 9 R strategies, ecodesign, and the new business models that make circularity work.

From linear to circular 2.1

Summary
  • The linear economy assumes resources are abundant/cheap and the planet can absorb unlimited waste — leading to finite-resource dependence, planned obsolescence and end-of-cycle waste.
  • The circular economy is a systemic model that keeps resources in use as long as possible, treating waste as a design flaw.
  • Ellen MacArthur Foundation — 3 principles (powered by renewable energy): ① Eliminate waste & pollution, ② Circulate products & materials (technical + biological cycles), ③ Regenerate nature.
  • Key idea: decouple growth/value from resource consumption; "in nature there is no waste — waste is a human invention".
Full detail

Linear economy — features & consequences

Resource extraction → processing & production → assembling → residue/end-of-life. It assumes natural resources are abundant, accessible and cheap and the environment can absorb unlimited waste. Features: dependence on finite resources, intensive production & accelerated consumption, GDP-based indicators, planned/perceived obsolescence and a throwaway culture, and waste at the end of the cycle (no material returns). "The linear economy is not only unsustainable: it is foolish on a finite planet."

Circular economy

A systemic economic model that moves away from "produce-use-throw away" and instead keeps resources in use as long as possible, reducing waste and pressure on the environment. It shares the sustainability vision but is focused on the economy & environment axes, oriented to production & consumption processes, and is operational/instrumental (an action guide).

The three principles (Ellen MacArthur Foundation)

  1. Eliminate waste & pollution — design products, materials and infrastructure so they can re-enter the economy after use. Treat waste as a design flaw.
  2. Circulate products & materials — keep them in use via maintenance, sharing, reuse, repair and (last resort) recycling in the technical cycle; biodegrade organic materials back to nature in the biological cycle.
  3. Regenerate nature — shift from extraction to improving natural environments & building biodiversity (e.g. regenerative agriculture restoring soils).

All three are powered by a shift to renewable energy & materials.

Fundamentals

  • New mindset: closed cycles, "waste does not exist".
  • Design for circularity; decouple growth from resource use.
  • Collaboration & systemic innovation; natural-systems regeneration.
  • Priority actions: sharing assets, reuse/second-hand, life extension (maintenance, durability, upgrading), remanufacturing, recycling, anaerobic digestion, dematerialisation (direct & indirect), new tech (3D printing).

Full detail

The circular-economy materials connect to the macro debate covered in The Lancet Planetary Health (post-growth):

Circular metrics: CTI & Circulytics 3

Summary
  • CTI (Circular Transition Indicators, WBCSD) is material-flow based: three pillars — circular Inflow, Outflow recovery potential (design), Outflow actual recovery. % material circularity = weighted average of % circular inflow & outflow.

Full detail

CTI — Circular Transition Indicators (WBCSD)

CTI is based on material flows through the company; design, procurement and recovery are the key levers. Its three pillars:

  1. Inflow — how circular (non-virgin or renewable/sustainably managed) are the resources sourced?
  2. Outflow — recovery potential — does the design make recovery technically feasible & economically viable (design for disassembly, repairability, recyclability, biodegradability)?
  3. Outflow — actual recovery — how much outflow does the company actually recover (closed-loop or open-loop schemes)?

Combined, these give the % material circularity = weighted average of % circular inflow and % circular outflow. The framework's modules:

ModuleWhat it measures
Close the LoopEffectiveness at closing material loops → % material circularity.

  • Outcomes — the actual circular performance of products, services and material/energy/water flows.

Goes beyond certification, aiming to make companies want to join a movement toward regenerative economies. B Corps follow rigorous standards across five areas (ESG + community/stakeholders + the social enterprise's purpose), with two impact levels (CSR practices/reports and supplier impact). They embed a legal requirement to consider workers, customers, suppliers, community and environment, using profit & growth as a means to positive impact. (Certification mechanics: see A2 — BIA, 80/200, 3-yr recert.)

Reporting: GRI (voluntary) vs CSRD (compulsory) 3

Summary
  • GRI (Global Reporting Initiative) — the leading voluntary framework: Universal + Sector + Topic standards; content principles (stakeholder inclusiveness, sustainability context, materiality, completeness) & quality principles (accuracy, balance, clarity, comparability, reliability, timeliness).
  • CSRD (Dir. EU 2022/2464) — compulsory; approved 10 Nov 2022, in force 5 Jan 2023; amends the NFRD (→ "EINF" in Spain). ESRS specify the data; phased rollout; built on double materiality.
  • Double materiality = impact materiality (company → world) + financial materiality (world → company value).
Full detail

Voluntary — GRI

An independent international organisation providing a common language to disclose impacts to multiple stakeholders. Three standard types:

  • Universal Standards — compulsory items & requirements for all.
  • Sector Standards — topics adapted to specific sectors.
  • Topic Standards — environmental/social/economic topics, reported if material.

Principles for report content: stakeholder inclusiveness, sustainability context, materiality, completeness. Principles for report quality: accuracy, balance, clarity, comparability, reliability, timeliness. Topic series: Economic 201–207, Environmental 301–308, Social 401–418. A material topic is one important enough that it's essential to report; emphasis reflects relative priority.

Compulsory — CSRD

  • Directive (EU) 2022/2464, approved 10 Nov 2022, in force 5 Jan 2023; transposed in all EU countries; amends the NFRD (2014/95) — in Spain the "Estado de Información No Financiera" (EINF).
  • ESRS (European Sustainability Reporting Standards) specify exactly what data must appear.
  • Disclosure structure: governance, strategy, IRO management (impacts, risks & opportunities), metrics & targets — all under double materiality.

Double materiality

  • Impact materiality — the company's impact on people & planet.
  • Financial materiality — the effect of people & planet on the company's value (dependencies & risks).

Three lenses: Impact (effect on environment/people), Dependencies (natural/capital resources the activity needs), Risk & opportunity (effect of social/environmental factors on financial performance). The Omnibus (Simplification) Package proposes to streamline these requirements.

The EU Taxonomy 4

Summary
  • A classification system (Regulation 2020/852, June 2020) defining which economic activities count as environmentally sustainable — a cornerstone of EU sustainable finance & the Green Deal.
  • Six environmental objectives; to qualify, an activity must ① substantially contribute to ≥1, ② do no significant harm (DNSH) to the others, ③ meet minimum social safeguards, ④ pass technical screening criteria.
  • Reported as % of sales, CapEx & OpEx aligned; linked to SFDR; aimed at net-zero by 2050 and at preventing greenwashing.
Full detail

"Taxonomy" is the science of classifying things into hierarchical groups by common characteristics (Greek taxis = order, nomos = law). The EU Taxonomy applies this to green finance: a common, science-based classification so companies, investors and governments know which activities truly contribute to the ecological transition and climate neutrality. It is set by Regulation (EU) 2020/852 (June 2020).

The six environmental objectives

  1. Climate change mitigation
  2. Climate change adaptation
  3. Sustainable use & protection of water and marine resources
  4. Transition to a circular economy
  5. Pollution prevention & control
  6. Protection & restoration of biodiversity & ecosystems

The four criteria for a "sustainable" activity

  1. Substantially contribute to at least one of the six objectives.
  2. Do no significant harm (DNSH) to any of the others.
  3. Comply with minimum social safeguards (e.g. labour rights).
  4. Meet the specific technical screening criteria set by the EU.

How it's used

  • Companies report the % of sales, CapEx and OpEx aligned with the Taxonomy (in their non-financial report).
  • It is directly linked to ESG and to the SFDR (Sustainable Finance Disclosure Regulation) for financial-market participants.
  • Part of the EU regulatory framework (Green Deal), aligned with a net-zero 2050 trajectory.
  • Purpose: identify & evaluate sustainable activities, mobilise public & private capital, standardise definitions and avoid greenwashing. Other countries (China, UK, South Africa, Colombia) are adopting their own taxonomies; the Omnibus Package proposes changes to disclosure obligations.

Self-check questions

Answer out loud or on paper first, then reveal. A mix of recall and "explain/compare" prompts — the shape of a mixed exam.

1 How does the Doughnut reframe the goal of the economy? Show
It replaces "maximise GDP growth" with "stay in the safe & just space": meet everyone's needs (the social foundation) without overshooting the ecological ceiling (planetary boundaries). A healthy economy is designed to thrive, not necessarily grow.
2 Distinguish the two scales of inequality and give a driver of each. Show
Within countries (income/employment gaps, gender & ethnic discrimination) and global (between countries/regions). Drivers: within → labour-market & educational disparities; global → colonialism & unequal trade/value chains. Measured by the Gini coefficient (from the Lorenz curve).
3 What distinguishes the Social Economy from (a) capitalist firms and (b) the public sector? Show
From capitalist firms: capital is not the dominant organising principle (people & purpose first, "one person one vote", surpluses by activity). From the public sector: it is private & autonomous, not state-run.
4 Compare CSR and B-Corp — how far does each change the firm? Show
CSR reforms behaviour of conventional firms (manage impacts; risk being reputational). B-Corp adds a verified certification (BIA ≥80/200, 5 areas, legal duty to stakeholders) — reforming capitalism from within. Open question for both: does the core business model change, or only legitimacy?
5 Why is degrowth "not a recession", and what is the green-growth debate? Show
A recession is an unplanned contraction; degrowth is a planned, equitable reduction of throughput in rich economies, focused on well-being. The debate: is "green growth" (efficiency) enough, or must affluent economies also cut absolute material/energy use? (Decoupling question.)
6 In the ECG, what replaces GDP, financial balance and financial profit? Show
GDP → Product of the common good (macro); financial balance → Balance of the common good (meso, the Common Good Balance Sheet); financial profit → Examination of the common good (micro). Profit becomes a means, not the aim.
7 Name the governing bodies of a cooperative and contrast with a corporation. Show
Cooperative: General Assembly (sovereign, one member one vote) → elects the Governing Council ("Consejo Rector"), plus an oversight/intervention body. Corporation: General Meeting of Shareholders (votes ∝ shares) → Board of Directors.
8 When is social innovation "transformative", and what are the three scaling directions? Show
Transformative when it changes practices, relations, institutions and economic logics — not a one-off fix. Scaling: out (replicate), up (policy/institutions), deep (values/culture).
9 Explain Marx's surplus value and commodity fetishism. Show
Surplus value: workers create more value than their wage; capitalists appropriate the surplus → profit = exploitation. Commodity fetishism: social relations appear as relations between things, and economic interest is put before all else, shaping an anxious, competitive society.
10 Contrast the linear and circular economy on three dimensions. Show
E.g. Flow: take-make-dispose vs closed loops · Waste: end-of-pipe problem vs design flaw to eliminate · Growth & resources: coupled vs decoupled (also energy: fossil vs renewable; design: obsolescence vs durability).
11 State the EMF three principles and the two material cycles. Show
① Eliminate waste & pollution · ② Circulate products & materials · ③ Regenerate nature (powered by renewables). Cycles: technical (maintain/reuse/repair/recycle) & biological (biodegrade back to nature).
12 Group the 9 R's; why is a lower R "better"? Show
Smarter use (R0 Refuse, R1 Rethink, R2 Reduce) → extend lifespan (R3 Reuse, R4 Repair, R5 Refurbish, R6 Remanufacture, R7 Repurpose) → useful materials (R8 Recycle, R9 Recover). Lower R = higher circularity (preserves more value & avoids reprocessing energy).
13 When would you use MET/METCO instead of an LCA? Show
Use MET/METCO for a quick, qualitative screen — introducing ecodesign, structuring the life cycle, exploring circularity, when no quantitative indicators exist. Not when you have quantitative targets, need external communication, or want a certification (then use LCA).
14 Explain servitisation and why it counters planned obsolescence. Show
Selling the use/service rather than the product, so ownership stays with the manufacturer. Because the maker keeps the asset, durable, repairable, long-lived products become more profitable — reversing the incentive to build for obsolescence.
15 Walk through the four phases of an LCA. Show
① Goal & Scope (objective + boundaries) → ② Inventory (LCI) (materials & operations) → ③ Impact Assessment (LCIA) (unit impact × inventory → categories/midpoint indicators) → ④ Interpretation (vs baselines/targets → improvement).
16 CTI vs Circulytics — what does each measure? Show
CTI (WBCSD): material-flow circularity — inflow, outflow recovery potential, actual recovery → % material circularity (+ value & impact modules). Circulytics (EMF): how deeply circularity is embedded in management/strategy beyond operations (Enablers + Outcomes).
17 Define double materiality and link it to the CSRD. Show
Impact materiality (company's effect on people/planet) + financial materiality (people/planet's effect on company value). It is the foundation of the CSRD (Dir. 2022/2464), specified via the ESRS.
18 List the EU Taxonomy's four criteria; what is DNSH? Show
① Substantially contribute to ≥1 of the 6 objectives · ② Do No Significant Harm to the others · ③ meet minimum social safeguards · ④ pass technical screening criteria. DNSH = the activity must not significantly damage any of the other environmental objectives.
19 Distinguish reporting, certification and rating, with an example. Show
Reporting = you publish your performance vs a standard (e.g. GRI). Certification = third-party label vs criteria (e.g. ISO 14001). Rating = a third party scores/ranks your ESG (e.g. EcoVadis).
